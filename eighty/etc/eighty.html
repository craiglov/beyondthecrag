The term protocol appears in multiple fields of activity. Most notably, in computer science, protocol refers to a set of rules which establish how data is communicated from one computer to another. Coincidentally, one of the most widely used protocols - Hypertext Transfer Protocol - is better known as HTTP. 1 Elsewhere, in politics and diplomacy, protocol denotes two distinct and interlinked concepts: firstly, referring to the first document of a meeting from which a formal diplomatic agreement is derived; secondly, the mutually accepted procedures and etiquette for all parties of a diplomatic pursuit. The latter definition has lent itself to general use, particularly in science and medicine (and, consequently, in pseudoscience and pseudo-medicine) to refer to any formalized set of procedures (see: treatment protocol, research protocol, etc.) Since late 2020, I have seen and heard this word countless times in all of its above applications. Over time, its definitions revealed themselves to be one and the same. A protocol sets parameters for the negotiation of getting what one wants: for instance, a message across, data, a resource, autonomy, access, or an answer to a question. These parameters serve as the language of the pursuit. Like the meeting whose minutes form the protocol document, however, the protocol itself is born from negotiation. Negotiation, obviously, necessitates compromise: in the case of the protocol as language (allowing parties to understand one another who previously could not), one or both parties must learn a language that is not their own. Regrettably, in many cases, seniority designates the better deal, as in the case of backwards compatibility: newer, more efficient models essentially “dumb themselves down” for successful communication with legacy systems.

With regard to all the protocols I have negotiated, adhered to, and had enacted upon me in the past three years, the necessity of either radical improvement or total overhaul has long been painfully obvious. Whilst waiting on proposed (inadequate) reforms to filter through long bureaucratic processes (more protocols holding in place protocols) it became clear that “slow progress” is not, in fact, superior to “no progress” when one is working in opposition to a target to whom any movement is data of the highest tactical advantage. I could no longer afford, therefore, to wait for my colleagues to format a message which was actively being distorted and weaponized against us in real time.

Maintaining that I was the primary system through which our project functioned, our protocol –the language of our pursuits–should have been based on my capabilities from its inception; instead, I was never so much as granted the opportunity to formalize my own means of communication (a fact which was repeatedly used against me in formal disputes). Personal Protocol is a long-overdue attempt at such a formalization.

“Protocol” is used here in every applicable sense of the word. As such, it should be treated both as a preliminary document and as a set of absolute rules; as both subject to radical change and as strictly non-negotiable. The Personal Protocol replaces a group of obsolete protocols pertaining to various media including but not limited to audio, video, images, text, brain activity, and the nervous system2 3; in light of this, it is both explicable and implementable through all of the above. It is informed by the most effective processes by which I as a system interface with in/external data. As a result, it will require other systems and parties to adapt to said processes: to speak my language. Extensive research and rigorous testing have determined this unequivocally as the most effective possible approach.

Gradual implementation of the Personal Protocol has now commenced its 2nd degree, Atlantis 2°, on this the 5th June, 2023. Personal Protocol will be fully available for public use on the 23rd June, 20234 , including 1 audiovisual and 7 audio-only modules, following a brief clinical trial delivered virtually and in-person on the 22nd June, 2023. I trust it will serve as a valuable resource.
